Paducah Wall to Wall

  • 1913 Flood

    Quick Facts:

    -During the 1913 flood, Paducah citizens watched in horror as the water overtook Louisville and continued to rise in their own town at a rate of 2 feet per day.

    -Before the water rose to critical levels, local bankers and government officials announced that Paducah would pay for its own recovery efforts and would not require the funds of outside agencies. -The crest of the flood was 54.3 feet -- one-tenth higher than that of 1884.

    -Local newspapers bragged that Paducahans didn't let this flood bother them any more than Paducahans of 1884 were bothered by that river rampage.

    -Thankfully, the city was prepared and escaped any significant damage. The end total cost of recovery was $6,000, which was paid by local funds.

    -Many took their boats out onto the water for joy rides during the height of the flood.

  • 1937 Flood

    Quick Facts:

    -The Red Cross noted that this natural disaster was bigger than the other two biggest disasters in recorded history in North America combined the Dust Bowl and the 1927 Mississippi River flood.

    -The previous high water mark set in 1913 was surpassed by six-and-a-half feet. The crest on February 2 measured 60.8 feet.

    -Conditions were worsened by winter weather

    -During the height of the flood, several buildings caught fire as the Fire Department looked on, helpless to stop it. Authorities evacuated the community during severe weather -- mostly due to the threat of illnesses like pneumonia and typhoid -- and military units were called in to assist.

    -Segregation was strictly maintained even during evacuation and recovery.

    -The recovery efforts were difficult and slow due to the weather and the severing of communication between Paducah and the outside world.

    -Bodies began appearing as the waters fell.

    -Paducah's business sector and population were devastated. Community growth came to a standstill until the construction of the floodwall.

    Personal 1937 Flood Stories:

    Ann Anderson Freeman and Dorothy Anderson Conner:

    During evacuation, their family's boat capsized. Ann, wrapped in blankets, began to float away and nearly drowned before a man jumped out of his own boat to save her. The sisters' visiting great grandmother, Addie Schiff, died in the struggle.

    This is something that mother and daddy never talked about, says Conner. We never talked about the flood and how terrible it was. They didn't have anything to say about it. Now, says Freeman, We don't really have anything to pass on to anybody else because we don't know that much about it.

    Rev. Robert M. Anderson

    Anderson was 17 when the flood occured and took shelter in a school. He was then evacuated to Princeton, Kentucky. The mayor of Princeton complained about the number of black refugees entering the city, so all of the black people in the group were transported by train to Knoxville, Tennessee. Guards were posted at the doors, and the refugees were not allowed to leave. After more than 2 weeks, people were returned to Paducah by train.

    We would eat, but that's all we would do‐set up in that building, couldn't get out, couldn't go no place. They had a guard on the door.

    I learned [from the flood] that anything could happen at any time.You're not safe no place.

  • Floodwall Contruction and Delay August 28, 1937

    Quick Facts:

    -The Flood Control Act was approved on August 28, 1937 by President Franklin Delano Roosevelt in response to the major flooding of the previous year leading up to the Super Flood of the Ohio River that swept Paducah.

    -This act approved funds of over $25 million in order to build floodwalls, levees, and revetments.

    -Paducah assured its cooperation with the act on May 2, 1938.

    -Construction lasted from August 1939 through July 1949.

    -Progress was delayed by World War II.

    -In January of 1950 a flood was prevented by the newly completed flood wall.

    -The part of the wall we see is 3 miles long (15,870 feet of concrete), but the entire structure is over 12 miles.

  • Maintenance and Restoration

    Quick Facts:

    -The Paducah Flood Protection Project is tasked with the upkeep of the wall.

    -In 1998, Congressman Whitfield requested $100,000 to conduct a study in order to determine if the restoration of the wall met federal requirements.

    -This study was completed in May 2000, and the total estimated cost of construction was $5.2 million.

    -Unfortunately, the money was tied up in bureaucracy and the legal definitions of “restoration” and “maintenance” were debated until 2005.

    -In 2006 after Hurricane Katrina hit, Paducah's floodwall was rated as Minimally Acceptable by USACE Louisville District.

    -The restoration project was unavoidable if the floodwall was going to continue in its current functions. However, government red tape blocked the money for years.

    -In December 2009 a preliminary restoration project was completed in order to secure federal funding for the rest of the project.

    -Restoration efforts are still ongoing and finding funding has been difficult.

    -In 2017 Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell announced that additional funding was being provided for restoration of the floodwall.

  • Murals

    Quick Facts:

    -The giant murals painted on the floodwall were created by Robert Dafford, a muralist from Lafayette, Louisiana, and the Dafford Murals Team.

    -They worked from the late 1990s to the early 2000s to complete the paintings.

    -The murals receive touch-ups from Dafford and his team every year or so to keep them looking new.

    -Each mural depicts a scene from Paducah's history from pre-Colonial times to the 1950s and adds a bit of whimsy to what would otherwise be a plain concrete wall.
